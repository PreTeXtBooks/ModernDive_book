    Section 10.2 Theory-based inference for simple linear regression

    This section introduces the conceptual framework needed for theory-based inference for regression and discusses the two most prominent methods for inference: confidence intervals and hypothesis tests. Some of this material is slightly more technical than other sections in this chapter, but most of the material is illustrated by working with a real example and interpretations and explanations complement the theory. Subsection 10.2.5 presents the R code needed to calculate relevant quantities for inference.
    🔗

    Subsection 10.2.1 Conceptual framework

    We start by reviewing the assumptions of the linear model. We continue using old_faithful_2024 to illustrate some of this framework. Since we assume a linear relationship between the duration of an eruption and the waiting time to the next eruption, we can express the linear relationship for the \(i\)th observation as \(y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot x_i + \epsilon_i\) for \(i=1,\dots,n\text{.}\) The error term \(\epsilon_i\) is a random variable that represents how different the observed response \(y_i\) is from the expected response \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot x_i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can illustrate the role of the error term using two observations from old_faithful_2024. We select the 49th and 51st observations using slice():
    🔗
    old_faithful_2024 |>
  slice(c(49, 51))
    Observe that the duration time is the same for both observations, but the response waiting time is different. Assuming that the linear model is appropriate, both responses can be expressed as:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y_{49} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot 236 + \epsilon_{49}\\ y_{51} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot 236 + \epsilon_{51}, \end{align*}
    but \(y_{49} = 139\) and \(y_{51} = 176\text{.}\) The difference in responses is due to the error term as it accounts for variation in the response not accounted for by the linear model.
    🔗
    In order to perform theory-based inference we require one additional assumption. We let the error term be normally distributed with an expected value (mean) equal to zero and a standard deviation equal to \(\sigma\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \epsilon_i \sim \text{Normal}(0, \sigma). \end{equation*}
    The response \(y_i\) (the waiting time for the \(i\)th eruption) is the sum of the constant \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot x_i\) and the normally distributed random variable \(\epsilon_i\text{.}\) Based on properties of random variables and the normal distribution, we can state that \(y_i\) is also a normally distributed random variable:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_i \sim \text{Normal}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i\,,\, \sigma) \end{equation*}
    for \(i=1,\dots,n\text{.}\) Since \(\epsilon_i\) and \(\epsilon_j\) are independent, \(y_i\) and \(y_j\) are also independent for any \(i \ne j\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In addition, as stated in Subsection 10.1.5, the least-squares estimator \(b_1\) is a linear combination of the random variables \(y_1, \dots, y_n\text{.}\) So \(b_1\) is also a random variable! The coefficient for the slope results from a particular sample of \(n\) pairs of duration and waiting times. If we collected a different sample of \(n\) pairs, the coefficient for the slope would likely be different due to sampling variation.
    🔗
    Because \(y_1, \dots, y_n\) are normally distributed and \(b_1\) is a linear combination of these random variables, \(b_1\) is also normally distributed:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} b_1 \sim \text{Normal} \left(\beta_1\,,\, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}\right). \end{equation*}
    Similarly, \(b_0\) is a linear combination of \(y_1, \dots, y_n\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} b_0 \sim \text{Normal} \left(\beta_0\,,\, \sigma\sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar x^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}\right). \end{equation*}
    We can also standardize the least-square estimators such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z_0 = \frac{b_0 - \beta_0}{\sigma\sqrt{\dfrac{1}{n} + \dfrac{\bar x^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}} \qquad\text{ and }\qquad z_1 = \frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}} \end{equation*}
    are the corresponding standard normal distributions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 10.2.2 Standard errors for least-squares estimators

    Recall that in Chapter 7 and Subsection 7.4.6 we discussed that, due to the Central Limit Theorem, the distribution of the sample mean \(\overline{X}\) was approximately normal with mean equal to the parameter \(\mu\) and standard deviation equal to \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\text{.}\) We then used the estimated standard error of \(\overline{X}\) to construct confidence intervals and hypothesis tests.
    🔗
    An analogous treatment is now used to construct confidence intervals and hypothesis tests for \(b_0\) and \(b_1\text{.}\) The standard deviations for \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) are constructed using the sample size \(n\text{,}\) the values of the explanatory variables, their means, and the standard deviation of \(y_i\) (\(\sigma\)). While most of these values are known to us, \(\sigma\) is typically not.
    🔗
    Instead, we estimate \(\sigma\) using the estimator of the standard deviation, \(s\text{,}\) introduced in Subsection 10.1.4. The estimated standard deviation of \(b_1\) is called the standard error of \(b_1\text{,}\) and it is given by:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} SE(b_1) = \frac{s}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}. \end{equation*}
    The standard error of \(b_1\) quantifies how much variation the estimator of the slope \(b_1\) may have for different random samples. The larger the standard error, the more variation we would expect in the estimated slope \(b_1\text{.}\) Similarly, the standard error of \(b_0\) is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} SE(b_0) = s\sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar x^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}. \end{equation*}
    When using the estimator \(s\) instead of the parameter \(\sigma\text{,}\) we are introducing additional uncertainty in our calculations. For example, we can standardize \(b_1\) using
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{b_1 - \beta_1}{SE(b_1)}. \end{equation*}
    Because we are using \(s\) to calculate \(SE(b_1)\text{,}\) the value of the standard error changes from sample to sample, and this additional uncertainty makes the distribution of the test statistic \(t\) no longer normal. Instead, it follows a \(t\)-distribution with \(n-2\) degrees of freedom. The loss of two degrees of freedom relates to the fact that we are estimating two parameters in the linear model: \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 10.2.3 Confidence intervals for the least-squares estimators

    A 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_1\) can be thought of as a range of plausible values for the population slope \(\beta_1\) of the linear relationship between duration and waiting times. In general, if the sampling distribution of an estimator is normal or approximately normal, the confidence interval for the relevant parameter is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{point estimate} \pm \text{margin of error} = \text{point estimate} \pm (\text{critical value} \cdot \text{standard error}). \end{equation*}
    The formula for a 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_1\) is given by \(b_1 \pm q \cdot SE(b_1)\) where the critical value \(q\) is determined by the level of confidence required, the sample size used, and the corresponding degrees of freedom for the \(t\)-distribution. For the Old Faithful example, with \(n - 2 = 112\) degrees of freedom, the critical value is \(q = 1.981\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The standard error for \(b_1\) using the formula presented earlier is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} SE(b_1) = \frac{s}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar x)^2}}. \end{equation*}
    We are 95% confident that the population slope \(\beta_1\) falls in this interval. The construction of a 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_0\) follows exactly the same steps using \(b_0\text{,}\) \(SE(b_0)\text{,}\) and the same critical value \(q\) as the degrees of freedom for the \(t\)-distribution are the same, \(n-2\text{.}\) The results of the confidence intervals are valid only if the linear model assumptions are satisfied, as discussed in Subsection 10.2.6.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 10.2.4 Hypothesis test for population slope

    To perform a hypothesis test for \(\beta_1\text{,}\) the general formulation of a two-sided test is:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} H_0&: \beta_1 = B\\ H_A&: \beta_1 \ne B, \end{align*}
    where \(B\) is the hypothesized value for \(\beta_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Test statistic. The \(t\)-test statistic is given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{b_1 - B}{SE(b_1)}. \end{equation*}
    This test statistic follows, under the null hypothesis, a \(t\)-distribution with \(n-2\) degrees of freedom. A particularly useful test is whether there is a linear association between the explanatory variable and the response:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} H_0&: \beta_1 = 0\\ H_A&: \beta_1 \ne 0. \end{align*}
    For the Old Faithful example, the null hypothesis \(H_0\) assumes that the population slope \(\beta_1\) is 0, meaning there is no linear relationship between duration and waiting times. The test statistic is:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{b_1 - 0}{SE(b_1)}. \end{equation*}
    The \(p\)-value. Recall the definition of the \(p\)-value: the probability of obtaining a test statistic just as extreme as or more extreme than the one observed, assuming the null hypothesis \(H_0\) is true. For a two-sided test, if the test statistic is \(t = 2\) for example, the \(p\)-value is the area under the \(t\)-curve to the left of \(-2\) and to the right of \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In the Old Faithful geyser eruptions example, the \(p\)-value was so small that R simply shows that it is equal to zero.
    🔗
    Interpretation. Since the \(p\)-value was practically 0, for any choice of significance level \(\alpha\text{,}\) we would reject \(H_0\) in favor of \(H_A\text{.}\) We reject the null hypothesis that there is no linear relationship between duration and waiting times. We have enough statistical evidence to conclude that there is a linear relationship between these variables.
    🔗

    Subsection 10.2.5 The regression table in R

    The least-square estimates, standard errors, test statistics, \(p\)-values, and confidence interval bounds discussed in Section 10.2 can be calculated all at once using the R wrapper function get_regression_table() from the moderndive package. For model_1:
    🔗
    get_regression_table(model_1)
    The first row in the regression table addresses inferences for the intercept \(\beta_0\text{,}\) and the second row deals with inference for the slope \(\beta_1\text{.}\) The columns present the following:
    🔗
    • The estimate column contains the least-squares estimates, \(b_0\) (first row) and \(b_1\) (second row).
      🔗
      🔗
    • The std_error contains \(SE(b_0)\) and \(SE(b_1)\text{,}\) the standard errors for \(b_0\) and \(b_1\text{,}\) respectively.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The statistic column contains the \(t\)-test statistic for \(b_0\) (first row) and \(b_1\) (second row), constructed using \(t = b_1 / SE(b_1)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The p_value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic just as extreme as or more extreme than the one observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The values lower_ci and upper_ci are the lower and upper bounds of a 95% confidence interval for \(\beta_1\text{.}\)
      🔗

    Subsection 10.2.6 Model fit and model assumptions

    We have introduced the linear model alongside assumptions about many of its elements and assumed all along that this is an appropriate representation of the relationship between the response and the explanatory variable. In real-life applications, it is uncertain whether the relationship is appropriately described by the linear model or whether all the assumptions we have introduced are satisfied.
    🔗
    We do not expect the linear model, or any other model, to be a perfect representation of a phenomenon presented in nature. Models are simplifications of reality in that they do not intend to represent exactly the relationship in question but rather provide useful approximations. This approach is known as the principle of parsimony or Occam’s razor.
    🔗
    There are four elements in the linear model we want to check. To help you remember them, we use the following acrostic:
    🔗
    1. Linearity of relationship between variables — Is the relationship between \(y_i\) and \(x_i\) truly linear for each \(i = 1, \dots, n\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Independence of each of the response values \(y_i\) — Are \(y_i\) and \(y_j\) independent for any \(i \ne j\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Normality of the error terms — Is the distribution of the error terms at least approximately normal?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Equality or constancy of the variance for \(y_i\) — Is the variance of the response \(y_i\) always the same, regardless of the fitted value or regressor value?
      🔗
      🔗
    This acrostic follows the word LINE . To check for Linearity, Normality, and Equal or constant variance, we use the residuals of the linear regression via residual diagnostics . To check for Independence, we can use the residuals if the data was collected in a time sequence, or otherwise independence may be achieved by taking a random sample.
    🔗

    Subsubsection 10.2.6.1 Residuals

    Recall that given a random sample of \(n\) pairs \((x_1, y_1), \dots, (x_n, y_n)\) the linear regression was given by \(\widehat{y}_i = b_0 + b_1 \cdot x_i\text{.}\) The residual is the observed response minus the fitted value:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e_i = y_i - \widehat{y}_i \quad\text{ for } i = 1, \dots, n. \end{equation*}
    Combining these two formulas we get \(y_i = \underbrace{b_0 + b_1 \cdot x_i}_{\widehat{y}_i} + e_i\text{,}\) which looks very similar to our linear model \(y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot x_i + \epsilon_i\text{.}\) In this context, residuals can be thought of as rough estimates of the error terms.
    🔗
    In Figure 10.2.2, we illustrate one particular residual for the Old Faithful geyser eruption. An arrow connects the observed waiting time (a circle) with the fitted waiting time (a square). The vertical distance between these two points is the value of the residual for this observation.
    🔗
    We can calculate all residuals by applying the get_regression_points() function to the regression model model_1:
    🔗
    # Fit regression model:
model_1 <- lm(waiting ~ duration, data = old_faithful_2024)
# Get regression points:
fitted_and_residuals <- get_regression_points(model_1)
fitted_and_residuals
    🔗

    Subsubsection 10.2.6.2 Residual diagnostics

    Residual diagnostics are used to verify conditions L, N, and E. One of the most useful plots is a residual plot, which is a scatterplot of the residuals against the fitted values. We use fitted_and_residuals to draw the scatterplot with the fitted values (waiting_hat) on the x-axis and the residuals (residual) on the y-axis, and add a horizontal line at 0:
    🔗
    ggplot(fitted_and_residuals, aes(x = waiting_hat, y = residual)) +
  geom_point() +
  labs(x = "duration", y = "residual") +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0, col = "blue")
    In Figure 10.2.3 we show this residual plot (right) alongside the scatterplot for duration vs waiting (left). Note how the residuals on the left plot are determined by the vertical distance between the observed response and the linear regression. On the right plot, the effect of the linear regression has been removed.
    🔗
    Linearity of relationship. We want to check whether the association between the response \(y_i\) and the explanatory variable \(x_i\) is Linear. A scatterplot of residuals against fitted values showing no patterns but simply a cloud of points that seems randomly assigned — called a null plot — does not show any evidence against the linearity assumption. This is largely the case for the Old Faithful geyser example.
    🔗
    By contrast, Figure 10.2.4 shows a simulated example where the residual plot has a U-shaped polynomial curve. If this was the real data observed, using the linear regression would produce results that are not valid.
    🔗
    Independence of the error terms and the response. Another assumption we want to check is the Independence of the response values. If data was collected in a time sequence, plotting residuals against time can help determine lack of independence. The old_faithful_2024 dataset contains a date variable. Figure 10.2.5 shows the plot of residuals against date.
    🔗
    The plot of residuals against time seems to be a null plot. Based on this plot we could say that the residuals do not exhibit any evidence of dependency. Since we can consider this a random sample, the assumption of independence seems acceptable.
    🔗
    Normality of the error terms. The third assumption we want to check is whether the error terms follow Normal distributions with expected value equal to zero. We can use a histogram and a quantile-to-quantile (QQ) plot of the residuals:
    🔗
    ggplot(fitted_and_residuals, aes(residual)) +
  geom_histogram(binwidth = 10, color = "white")

    fitted_and_residuals |>
  ggplot(aes(sample = residual)) +
  geom_qq() +
  geom_qq_line()
    The QQ-plot creates a scatterplot of the quantiles of the residuals against the quantiles of a normal distribution. If the residuals follow approximately a normal distribution, the scatterplot would be a straight line of 45 degrees. Figure 10.2.6 shows both plots for model_1.
    🔗
    The histogram does not appear exactly normal but does not deviate much. The QQ-plot supports this conclusion. For comparison, Figure 10.2.7 shows residuals from a simulated model that clearly do not follow normality.
    🔗
    Equality or constancy of variance for errors. The final assumption we check is the Equality or constancy of the variance — homoskedasticity — for the error term across all fitted values or regressor values. We check that the dispersion of the residuals is the same for any fitted value \(\widehat{y}_i\) or regressor \(x_i\text{:}\)
    🔗
    ggplot(fitted_and_residuals, aes(x = duration, y = residual)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.6) +
  labs(x = "duration", y = "residual") +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0)
    The spread of the residuals is not exactly constant across all values of duration, but there is not clear evidence against the assumption of homoskedasticity. For comparison, Figure 10.2.9 shows a simulated example with non-constant variance.
    🔗
    Lack of constant variance is also known as heteroskedasticity. When heteroskedasticity is present, the standard error of the least-square estimators, confidence intervals, or the conclusion for a related hypothesis test would not be valid.
    🔗
    Conclusion. For the Old Faithful data, we did not find conclusive evidence against any of the assumptions of the model: Linearity, Independence, Normality, and Equality of variance. When there are clear violations, remedial measures include variable transformations for non-linearity, linear mixed-effects models for non-independence, and weighted least squares for non-constant variance.
    🔗
    Checking that the assumptions of the model are satisfied is a key component of regression analysis. It is important to highlight important elements that may suggest departures from relevant assumptions, and then provide pertinent conclusions.
